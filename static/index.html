YOUR OPEN POSITIONS — EXPLAINED

Every time DotVerse gives you a signal and you enter a trade, record it here. This table keeps track of every trade you currently have open, so the Risk Manager below can calculate your real exposure.

WHAT EACH FIELD MEANS:
TICKER — The asset symbol. AAPL = Apple stock. BTC-USD = Bitcoin. EURUSD=X = Euro vs Dollar.
SIGNAL — BUY means you expect the price to go up. SELL (short) means you expect it to go down. Only log what you actually traded.
ENTRY PRICE — The exact price you paid when you opened the trade. Get this from your broker's order confirmation.
STOP PRICE — The price where you'll exit if the trade goes against you. This is your safety net. DotVerse calculated this from ATR (volatility). Never skip it.
TP1 PRICE — Take Profit 1. The first price target where you plan to take some profit. This comes from the signal card.
SIZE% — What % of your total account is in this one trade. Example: $10,000 account, 5% size = $500 in this trade. Keep each trade under 5%. This way, even if it goes to zero, you only lose 5%.
TF (Timeframe) — The chart timeframe you used for the signal. 1D = daily candles (swing trade, days to weeks). 4H = 4-hour candles (short-term, hours to days). 1H or 15M = day trading.
Rule of thumb: If your total SIZE% across all positions adds up to more than 20–30%, you're over-exposed. A single bad market day could wipe a large chunk of your account.
Parametric Value-at-Risk (VaR)
Add positions above, then compute VaR.
VALUE-AT-RISK — EXPLAINED

VaR answers one question: "On a typical bad day, how much could I lose from my entire portfolio?"

THE INPUTS:
Portfolio Value ($) — Enter your total account size in dollars. If you have $10,000 in your trading account, type 10000.
Confidence Level — Choose how strict the estimate should be:
95% — On 95 out of 100 normal trading days, your loss stays below this number. Good for daily monitoring.
99% — Only 1 day in 100 should exceed this loss. More conservative. Use this if you're very risk-averse.
THE RESULT:
Portfolio STD — Standard Deviation. This is how volatile your portfolio is day-to-day. Higher = wilder swings. A crypto-heavy portfolio will have a much higher STD than a stock-only portfolio.
VaR Amount — The dollar amount you could lose on a bad day. Example: VaR of $246 on a $10,000 account = 2.46% daily risk.
WHAT'S A SAFE LEVEL?
Below 1% — Very safe. Conservative portfolio.
1–2% — Acceptable. Normal trading risk.
2–4% — Getting risky. Consider reducing size.
Above 4% — Dangerous. One bad day could hurt badly.
Note: VaR does NOT predict crashes. It's based on normal market behaviour. The Stress Test below handles crash scenarios.

STRESS TEST — EXPLAINED

This is a crash simulation. It asks: "If markets dropped hard tomorrow, how much money would I lose?"

THE SHOCK LEVELS (WHY THESE NUMBERS?):
Crypto −30% — Bitcoin dropped 33% in one week in May 2021. −30% is a realistic bad week for crypto. It's highly volatile.
Stocks −20% — The S&P 500 fell 34% in March 2020 (COVID crash) and 19% in 2022. −20% represents a severe but plausible drop.
Forex −10% — Currency pairs rarely swing more than 5–10% in a crisis. The GBP/USD fell 10% on Brexit night.
Index −15% — Major indices (Nasdaq, FTSE) commonly fall 15% during corrections and recessions.
Commodity −20% — Oil dropped 30% in a single day in 2020. Gold is more stable but can still swing 15–20%.
READING THE RESULTS:
Alloc% — How much of your portfolio is in that asset. Example: Alloc 5% means $500 of a $10,000 account is in AAPL.
Stressed P&L — If the crash happened right now, this is what you'd see in your account in dollar terms.
WHAT SHOULD I DO WITH THIS?
Total loss < 5% — You'd survive a crash with minimal damage. Safe.
5–15% loss — Painful but survivable. Review your largest positions.
15–30% loss — Dangerous. Reduce position sizes before a crash happens.
Above 30% loss — Your account is at serious risk. Close some trades immediately.
Tip: Run this before you add a new position — not after. Prevention beats recovery.

CORRELATION — EXPLAINED

Correlation tells you how much two assets move together. The higher the number, the more they copy each other's movements.

THE SCALE (−1 TO +1):
+1.0 (dark green) — Perfect sync. When one goes up 5%, the other goes up 5% too. Holding both offers zero protection.
+0.5 to +0.8 — Move together most of the time. Some protection, but not much. Common between BTC and ETH.
0.0 (neutral) — Completely unrelated. Great diversification. Example: a US stock vs a Japanese yen pair.
−1.0 (dark red) — Perfect opposites. When one crashes, the other rallies. Rare but ideal for hedging risk.
REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE:
BTC and ETH typically have a correlation of ~0.85. This means if you hold both, you're effectively just holding one risky asset with extra steps. In a crypto crash, they both fall together.
HOW TO READ THE HEATMAP:
• The diagonal (top-left to bottom-right) is always 1.0 — every asset is 100% correlated with itself.
• Focus on the off-diagonal cells — those show how your holdings relate to each other.
• A good portfolio has mostly grey/pale cells off the diagonal. Lots of dark green = you're not actually diversified.
WHAT TO DO:
Avg correlation < 0.2 — Well diversified. A crash in one sector won't kill all positions.
0.2–0.5 — Moderate. Some overlap but reasonable.
Above 0.5 — Too similar. Consider replacing one correlated pair with an unrelated asset (e.g. gold, a forex pair, or an index).
Tip: Try adding GC=F (Gold) or EURUSD=X to your ticker list. Gold often moves opposite to stocks during a panic — that's why it's called a safe haven.

OPTIMISATION — EXPLAINED

This is the strategy tuner. It automatically tests hundreds of indicator combinations on 2 years of real market data to find which settings would have performed best — then gives you the winning settings to use.

THE INDICATORS IT TESTS:
RSI Period — RSI measures if an asset is overbought (price went up too fast) or oversold (price dropped too far). The "period" is how many candles it looks back. RSI 10 = faster, more signals, better for fast-moving crypto. RSI 21 = slower, fewer signals, better for stable markets like indices.
ATR Multiplier — ATR (Average True Range) measures how much a price normally moves per day. The multiplier sets how wide your stop loss is. ATR 2× = tighter stop, more trades but more false exits. ATR 4× = wider stop, fewer exits but each stop is further away.
EMA Pair — EMA (Exponential Moving Average) is a smoothed average of recent prices. The pair (e.g. 9/21) uses two averages — when the fast one crosses above the slow one, it's a buy signal. 7/14 = very fast (day trading). 20/50 = slow (swing trading).
THE SCORE — SHARPE RATIO:

Sharpe measures how much profit you earned per unit of risk taken. Think of it as: "Was the profit worth the rollercoaster?"

4.0+ — Exceptional — Rare. These settings delivered excellent returns with low volatility.
2.0–4.0 — Strong — Great settings. Use these.
1.0–2.0 — Decent — Acceptable. A Sharpe of 1 means you earned roughly as much as the risk you took.
Below 1.0 — Weak — Not worth the risk on this asset class and timeframe.
HOW TO USE THE RESULT:
After you click Check Result, you'll see the winning RSI period, ATR multiplier, and EMA pair. Go to the Signals tab, run analysis on your asset, and DotVerse will automatically use those optimised settings for that asset class.
It takes ~2 minutes to complete. Click Enqueue Job, wait, then click Check Result. Run it once per asset class — results are saved and reused automatically.
